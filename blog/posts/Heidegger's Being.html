The Question of Being: A Personal Journey Through Heidegger's Labyrinth

November 16, 2024 8 min read

Late one night, I found myself consumed by a question that has haunted philosophers for millennia: What does it mean to be? Not to be something in particular like a writer, a thinker, a human being but simply to be at all. This is the question that consumed great philosopher Martin Heidegger too, and as I've spent a lot of time wrestling with his thought, I've come to believe it might be the only question that truly matters.

The Vertigo of Being

I remember the first time I truly grasped what Heidegger meant by the "forgetting of being." I was writing in my notebook, this ordinary object I use every day, when suddenly its sheer existence struck me with extraordinary force. Not its properties or its color, weight, or function but the sheer, overwhelming fact that it is. This experience, this vertigo of Being, is what Heidegger tried to recover from beneath centuries of philosophical abstraction.

Now to a normal person it will sound as too poetic or just romanticism. We've become so accustomed to treating Being as self-evident that we've lost our capacity for astonishment at the simple fact that things are. In our rush to analyze, categorize, and control beings, we've forgotten to wonder about Being itself. This forgetting isn't just some academic oversight; I've come to see it as the root of our modern crisis, our sense of disconnection from reality itself.

This was addressed by Heidegger too; in the first paragraph of his book Being and Time, he said:

"It (the Question of Being) sustained the avid research of Plato and Aristotle but from then on ceased to be heard as a thematic question of actual investigation."

The Mirror of Dasein

Heidegger's main idea is captured in the term Dasein, which means "being there." This concept emphasizes that human existence is deeply connected to the world around us.

When Heidegger speaks of Dasein human existence he's not just offering another anthropological theory. Through weeks of engaging with his work, I've come to see something profound. We are the beings who serve as a mirror for Being itself. This isn't ego-centric philosophy; it's quite the opposite. We are the clearing, the opening, the space where being can reveal itself.

Think about it right now, as you read these words, you're not just processing information. You're participating in a miracle of understanding, a moment where being itself becomes manifest through meaning. This is what makes us unique—not our rationality or consciousness, but our ability to be the site where Being shows up as meaningful.

The Hidden Depths of the Everyday

One of my most startling realizations about Heidegger's thought came while watching garba. The dancers don't approach the world as a collection of neutral objects waiting to be categorized. They're immediately immersed in a world of meaning; a cardboard box is already a spaceship, a stick already a sword. This isn't primitive thinking waiting to be corrected by adult rationality but it's a more primordial understanding of how we actually encounter being.

I've started to see how this applies everywhere. When I write, my keyboard isn't first encountered as an object with certain properties. It withdraws into its function, participating in a whole web of meanings and purposes. This isn't just true for tools; it's true for everything we encounter. The world isn't a collection of neutral objects that we later imbue with meaning; meaning is there from the start.

Time as the Horizon of Being

Perhaps my most radical insight into Heidegger's thought came during a period of loneliness. I realized that time isn't just a framework in which we exist; it's the very fabric of our being. When we're anxious, we're not just worried about future events but we're confronting the fundamental temporality of our existence.

Think about any moment of your life. Aren't you always already ahead of yourself, planning, hoping, fearing? Aren't you always carrying your past with you, not as baggage but as the very ground of who you are? And isn't the present always rich with meanings that come from both past and future? This temporal structure isn't something added to our existence; it is our existence and we are made by this structure.

The Technology of Being

Living in our digital age has given me a fresh appreciation for Heidegger's warnings about technology. The real danger isn’t the machines themselves; it’s the mindset that reduces everything—including ourselves—to mere resources to be optimized. Just think about it! We’ve transformed social relationships into "networking," boiled education down to "skill acquisition," and turned nature into just another "resource."

But wait, there’s more! This reductionist thinking is dangerous because it oversimplifies the beautifully complex nature of humanity. We constantly shove people into rigid, dichotomous categories, ignoring the rich nuances that make us who we are. Whether we’re glued to the news, grinding away at work, or lost in daydreams, this oversimplification creeps in everywhere.

But here's what I think Heidegger missed: technology might also offer new ways of revealing Being. When I see how digital art can create new spaces of meaning, how virtual reality can expand our understanding of presence, how artificial intelligence raises new questions about consciousness and Being, today, we can develop neural networks and see how they behave with some math. I wonder if we're not entering a new epoch in the history of being itself.

The Poetry of Being

One of my most controversial interpretations of Heidegger is that philosophy must become more like poetry. Not in style, but in its fundamental approach to truth. Poetry doesn't just describe reality; it lets reality reveal itself in new ways. When a poet speaks of a "silent thunder," they're not being fanciful; they're letting us encounter a truth that literal language can't capture.

I believe this is what thinking about being requires: not just new concepts, but new ways of letting language speak. We need to develop a way of thinking and speaking that doesn't try to grasp being as an object but lets it show itself in its own terms.

The Ethics of Being

Here's where I part ways with traditional Heidegger - I believe his thinking about being has profound ethical implications. If we understand ourselves as the "shepherds of Being," as he suggests, this comes with responsibility. Not just to ourselves or other people, but to being itself.

This means developing an approach to life that isn't based on rules or consequences, but on letting beings be in their fullness. This involves a profound respect for the mystery of existence, a willingness to let things reveal themselves in their own way, and a resistance to the technological impulse to control, optimize, and simplify everything.

The Future of Being

As I look to the future, I see both danger and hope. The danger is that we'll continue to forget being, that we'll lose ourselves entirely in the manipulation of beings. The hope is that this very forgetting might lead to a new remembering, that our technological age might give birth to a new way of encountering being.

Perhaps the ecological crisis, the challenges of artificial intelligence, and the questions raised by quantum physics, all these might force us to think again about what it means to be. Maybe we're on the cusp of a "new epoch of being," where the old metaphysical categories no longer hold and we're called to think in radically new ways.

A Personal Conclusion

After years of dwelling with these questions, I've come to see that thinking about being isn't just an academic exercise; it's a way of life. It means maintaining a constant openness to wonder, a willingness to let things be in their mystery, a resistance to the temptation to reduce everything to what we can control and understand.

The question of being might seem abstract, but I've found it to be the most concrete question of all. It touches everything we are and do. In asking about being, we're not just engaging in philosophy but we're engaging in the fundamental task of human existence, which is letting reality show itself in its inexhaustible richness.

As I write these final words, I'm aware that I haven't "explained" being. That wasn't my goal. Instead, I hope I've awakened in you what Heidegger called "the question worthiness of Being"—the sense that beneath all our everyday concerns lies an endless mystery that's worth thinking about. For in the end, the question of being isn't just about understanding reality; it's about learning to dwell poetically on this earth, in openness to the wonder that anything is at all.
