The Test of Independence: Nietzschean perspective

November 12, 2024 Prateek 8 min read

On this page

    Nietzsche is always all about Independence freedom and non-attachment but how to know that you are independent. What are the things people miss when they set out on the path to be truely independent. Is anyone truely independent? we’ll also see if anyone really needs this intense kind of freedom (or if it’s just for philosophers who’ve had one too many espressos)

    For Nietzsche, being “independent” wasn’t about getting your own place or deleting your ex’s number. He saw independence as a life test, something so demanding and dangerous it might leave you questioning your sanity. Here is how to approach this idea

    The self test

    Independence must be tested to be real. That too not by some external person or event but by diving deep in your own mind. These tests are internal and challenging, revealing our deepest truths. Each of us must set up our own trials and confront our own doubts, without expecting an external audience or validation. This is especially a big challenge today as we don't have much time to have that conversation with ourselves. To go to a walk alone and think about all this stuff.

    Facing Danger Alone

    For Nietzsche, these personal tests are far from safe—they are, in fact, dangerous. They challenge us to look beyond our comforts, face parts of ourselves we may usually ignore and usually result in an existential crises. These are risks we take with no safety net, with only our self-respect as a witness. True independence comes from a willingness to engage with this danger rather than avoiding it.

    People are Prisons (Even the ones you love)

    A major theme in Nietzsche’s philosophy is freedom from attachments, even today a lot people will ask you to avoid attachments even to your dearest relationships. When we cleave to someone, we risk becoming imprisoned by that bond. Every individual, however beloved, represents a world of views, limits, and expectations that may restrict our own growth. Independence means we can love others without needing to be their prisoner

    Say No to Sympathy (Especially for People You Admire)

    We all know the feeling: seeing someone admirable struggle and feeling the pull to help. But Nietzsche sees this sympathy as another way of losing ourselves. Lots of times when we start to help someone, we also sacrifice working towards our own vision. We can support people, yes, but we shouldn’t lose our own sense of purpose by merging with their pain

    Freedom from Scientific Attachments

    This might sound weird to you but Nietzsche wants us to avoid getting totally absorbed by any pursuit, even if it’s intellectually stimulating. Science, no matter how dazzling, shouldn’t monopolize our minds. True independence, he says, is the ability to dip into knowledge without drowning in it.

    Avoiding the Trap of Freedom

    This sounds paradoxical—Nietzsche cautions that the very pursuit of absolute freedom can become its own attachment. Imagine a bird flying ever higher, seeking infinite space but ultimately risking isolation and directionlessness. Freedom, Nietzsche implies, should not become an obsession but rather be balanced with grounding principles that prevent us from losing ourselves in endless exploration. In few words, freedom is important but it should be confined with the guardrails of sanity

    Don’t Become a Virtue Machine

    Surprise! Nietzsche doesn’t even want you to be too attached to your virtues. It is cool to tell your friends that you have a code or you are kind but these shouldn't define you entirely. Take generosity: giving everything might feel noble, but it can leave you feeling empty. Use your virtues, don't let them use you

    Art of self conservation

    Self-preservation is key here. Nietzsche insists that independence requires a bit of selfishness—not the self-centered kind, but the self-protective kind. This means managing our energy, time, and resources wisely, and not overextending ourselves in any one area. Independence requires self-care and a degree of restraint to keep us aligned with our highest goals and values.

    Who’s Really Independent? And Is This the Independence We Even Want?

    This vision of independence is radical, maybe even extreme. It’s not the everyday “freedom” we talk about, like choosing our jobs or deciding where to live. It’s independence from every possible external anchor—people, ideologies, even virtues—that might limit us. This brings us to question

    Is anyone really that free ?

    No one can be really that free, at the end we are humans and we have our flaws but what I think is that it’s worth striving for if we want a life that’s truly ours. Maybe the takeaway is this: Nietzsche’s independence isn’t about cutting ties recklessly. It’s about making sure that those ties don’t own us, that we can walk away when we need to.

    In the end almost everything that Nietzsche says is really about thinking, confronting yourself with no filters. The real question here is: are you ready to see what lies beneath the surface, or are you just playing at independence?
