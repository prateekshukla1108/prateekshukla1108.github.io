How to avoid burnout while working hardcore remotely

December 9, 2024 Prateek 8 min read

On this page

    When Coronavirus struck in 2020, almost everyone had to work from home, and soon we saw a huge rise in depression, anxiety, and other mental health issues in many people. Working remotely is not easy for someone unaccustomed to being alone. This is one of the reasons people stopped working alone, and as soon as the lockdown was lifted, almost everyone started working in offices again.

    Now, even though working alone can be stressful and tough, there are some strategies you can implement to improve your experience. I have personally been working alone for 10 months now and have faced almost all the struggles that come with it. Through my experience, I have developed many coping abilities to be more productive, efficient, and positive each day. Here are some of them:

    1. Compartmentalize Everything

    The biggest difference between working remotely and in an office is the lack of compartmentalization. When we work alone, we often start multitasking and lose focus, whereas in a professional environment, we know exactly what to do, and working time is dedicated solely to work. If you want to increase your productivity at home, compartmentalize your day aggressively while keeping it realistic.

    Always strategize the next day and examine the current day one hour before going to sleep. Keep your goals clear, concise, and realistic. Also, focus on one thing you could improve that day and apply that advice the next day.

    2. Use Pomodoro Technique

    Even if you are the Elon Musk of productivity, I recommend using the Pomodoro technique. It is the best way to focus on anything. It is measurable, allows you to make consistent progress, and makes it easier to avoid procrastination when you see 40 minutes of work rather than 10 or 12 hours.

    Always use a timer for Pomodoro. It might take a little time to set up, but once you are habituated, focusing consistently becomes much easier.

    3. Use NSDR

    NSDR stands for Non-Sleep Deep Rest. It is a method of completely relaxing your body without falling asleep. It helps recover sleep loss, reduce anxiety, and improve focus.

    I use NSDR whenever I am learning a lot and struggling to retain information. After just 10 minutes, I feel more energized, focused, and learn more efficiently. If you are feeling tired or fatigued, I recommend using NSDR.

    How to Use NSDR: I shut off all the lights, set an alarm for 10 minutes, and lie down on the floor. I avoid the bed because I usually fall asleep quickly. Since there is typically little noise in my room, I easily slip into relaxation.

    4. Embrace the Pain

    Greatness is forged in adversity, not comfort. The pain you endure now will pay off in the future. We all know under what conditions humans have grown the most, and they are not positions of comfort. If you constantly resent pain, you will feel more pain.

    Instead of resenting pain, embrace it. Take it as something that shapes the future version of you. Embracing pain fosters resilience. Each time we face and navigate through discomfort, we build our capacity to handle future challenges. You may still hate pain, but now it will be linked to a greater purpose. Greatness requires harsh environments that test us.

    5. Exercise, Cold Showers, and Cleaning the Workspace

    One of the biggest ways to avoid burnout is doing physically challenging tasks. Working alone means sitting for hours in front of a computer, tinkering with ideas, or solving problems. It's easy to forget the physical body that sustains this mental marathon. But trust me—neglecting it is a recipe for burnout. Dedicate time to physical activity. It doesn't have to be a grueling workout; sometimes it's a brisk walk, a quick yoga session, or a 10-minute workout. The goal isn't just fitness—it's priming your mind.

    Cold showers: two words that make most people cringe, but for me, they're a non-negotiable part of my day. Why? Because they're the ultimate mental reset. They jolt your system and force you to confront discomfort. That's the magic. When you start your day by voluntarily facing something uncomfortable, everything else feels more manageable. The physical benefits are undeniable: huge dopamine increase, improved circulation, reduced inflammation, and a burst of energy that caffeine can't replicate.

    Cleaning your workspace is important because it makes it easier to start working. It's more of an efficiency measure than a behavioral one. If you keep everything related to work accessible, it reduces the physical friction to begin working.

    6. Use Reinforcement Learning

    Reinforcement Learning (RL), a paradigm inspired by behavioral psychology, involves learning through rewards and penalties. The idea is to reward desired behaviors immediately after completion to strengthen the connection between the action and the reward, and to apply small, immediate penalties for undesired behaviors to deter repetition.

    For example:

    • After completing a focused work session, allow yourself some relaxation time or a favorite treat.
    • If you procrastinate, reduce your leisure time by 10 minutes or skip a planned reward for that day.

    Reward yourself only when completing tasks during pre-defined focus times. Avoid rewards for tasks done outside this window to enforce discipline. Keep penalties mild to avoid discouragement. The goal is to correct, not demotivate. You can also mix the timing and intensity of rewards to keep the process engaging and reduce the risk of monotony. Occasionally surprise yourself with unexpected rewards for particularly good performance to keep motivation high.

    Conclusion: Your Remote Work Journey

    Remote work is not just a challenge—it's an opportunity for personal growth and self-discovery. The strategies outlined here are not a one-size-fits-all solution, but rather a toolkit from which you can select and adapt techniques that resonate with your unique working style and personality.

    Remember, the transition to effective remote work is a process, not an overnight transformation. Be patient with yourself. Some days will be more productive than others, and that's okay. The key is consistency, self-awareness, and a willingness to experiment and improve.

    Start small. You don't need to implement all these strategies at once. Choose one or two that feel most approachable and gradually build your remote work resilience. Whether it's adopting the Pomodoro technique, practicing NSDR, or creating a more structured daily routine, each small step is a victory.

    Ultimately, successful remote work is about creating an environment and mindset that allows you to thrive, not just survive. It's about finding a balance that keeps you motivated, healthy, and productive—no matter where your office might be. Embrace the journey, be kind to yourself, and keep pushing forward.
